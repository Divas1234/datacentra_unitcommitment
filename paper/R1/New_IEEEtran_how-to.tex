\begin{abstract}
  Data center clusters (DCCs) have become recognized as significant energy consumers in electrical power system operations. Workload reallocation processes for multiple DCCs, implemented by dynamic voltage and frequency scaling (DVFS) control schemes, can provide flexibility potential capabilities and contribute to maintaining the security of power systems operations through Unit Commitment (UC). To achieve this,
The flexibility potential of workload reallocation hosted by multiple DCCs is analyzed. Building on this analysis, an interval-based workload reallocation strategy is extended to depict flexibility potential from workload response while avoiding service-time latency. Then, an innovation set of physical security constraints representing DVFS-controlled DCCs is developed, and related special-ordered sets-of-type-1 ~(SOS1) linearized constraints are derived through the McCormick Envelopes method. Furthermore, these intractable constraints are incorporated into UC, and an enhanced UC model, which considers the flexibility potential offered by DVFS-controlled DCCs, is developed. Several experiments are conducted on the IEEE 6 buses test systems to assess the validity and scalability of the proposed models.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{A}{s} the increase in various artificial intelligence technologies and demand energy consumption, the operation of the electrical power system is becoming diverse and uncontrollable. It faces challenges in providing sufficient flexible adjustment resources against emergence events~\cite{zhou2025data}. In such circumstances, additional flexible mechanisms, such as demand response (DR), virtual power plant (VPP), and battery energy storage systems (BESS), need to be prioritized. Nevertheless, as a significant energy consumer, DCCs have not been fully utilized in the flexibility potential of power system operations. To this end, it is essential to investigate the flexible adjustment characteristic of DCCs and estimate their flexibility potential for the economical and reliable operation of electrical power systems.

Recent research has reported considerable advancements in understanding the potential of DCCs to enhance flexibility within electrical power systems. Specially,
Ref.~\cite{10874152} carried out a quality-of-service-based cost function to describe the workload shifting and server utilization of DCCs, This study is further extended a distributed dispatch framework to integrate DCCs flexibility.
Ref.~\cite{6041050} addressed the optimal demand management challenge among DCC servers  through a well-designed mix-integer programming model.
Ref.~\cite{7039172} proposed two optimization models to estimate the demand response capacities of DCCs.
Ref.~\cite{10806734} explored the coupling relationship between DCCs and hydrogen resources in microgrid systems and subsequentially carried out a data-driven rolling optimization model so as to ensure the optimal operation of DCCs and hydrogen storage systems.
Ref.~\cite{doi:10.1049/icp.2025.0664} analyses the flexibility potential of DCCs while concurrently reducing carbon emissions, culminating in an emission-aware coordinated dispatch model for multi-regional microgrids.
While these studies highlight the flexibility of DCCs within power systems, the intricate physical security characteristics associated with DCCs were not detailed.

The workloads in DCCs can be broadly categorized into three types,~\cite{6041050},~$i$) $compute$-$intensive$ $workloads$, which include high-performance computing, machine learning model training, and commercial application services. $ii$) $storage$-$intensive$ $workloads$, which are employed for such activities such as regular file backups, archival systems, and relational database applications. $iii$) $network$-$intensive$ $workloads$, these jobs encompass activities like website hosting, content delivery networks, and network traffic management. 
Notably, compute-intensive and storage-intensive workloads dominate DCCs, collectively accounting for approximately $70$\% of the total workload amount,~\cite{wangClusterFrameworksEfficient2018}. Moreover, the growing demand for high-performance computing and data-driven techniques has been contributing to this dominance. In contrast, network-intensive workloads are typically less predictable and highly time-sensitive, requiring immediate processing to ensure seamless operations. 
Since compute-intensive and storage-intensive workloads are predictable and flexible, they can be effectively shifted and reallocations to decrease their peak requirements of power consumpition. Techniques like DVFS allow for shifting workloads or responding to delays, leading to improved spatial-temporal workload balancing and overall energy management.

In addition to possible power management in DCCs through DVFS, it is essential to consider DCCs' physical security constraints related to job size and service-time latency. These factors directly impact the feasibility of workload reallocation and shifting processes between multiple DCC servers,~\cite{yu2023demand,wang2020flexible}. 
For instance, excessively shifting time-sensitive workloads between DCCs may lead to data congestion and latency risks. To reflect on this issue,
Ref.~\cite{kuehnDVFSpowerManagementPerformance2019} investigated a cloud-based DCCs queuing model using DVFS load-dependent strategies.
Ref.~\cite{hanDualtimeScaleCollaborative2025} analyzed the response characteristics of various time-tolerant (workload) jobs concerning service time and total energy consumption, proposing a dual-timescale collaborative optimization model for multi-DCC systems.
Ref.~\cite{dingEmissionawareStochasticResource2018} developed an emission-aware stochastic optimization model for microgrid system planning considering batch workload scheduling processes.
Ref.~\cite{hanDatadrivenDistributionallyRobust2025} extended a data-driven distributionally robust optimization model for low-carbon DCC systems, addressing multi-tasking responses and uncertainties in renewable energy integration.
Ref.~\cite{wangClusterFrameworksEfficient2018} provided a comprehensive survey of efficient task scheduling and resource allocation methodologies in multi-DCC networks.
Ref.~\cite{lin2023workload} proposed a workload-aware robust DVFS technique, Gear-DVFS, to address concurrency challenges in mobile-driven workloads.
Ref.~\cite{8520806} proposed a distributed shortest remaining time first (SRTF) scheduling model for DCCs workload reallocation, designed to minimize flow completion time while ensuring flow-size availability.
Ref.~\cite{wangSchedulingMachineLearningBasedFlow2018} constructed a machine-learning-based scheduling flow detection and packet-switched optical network (PSON) architecture for intra-DCC connectivity and bath workload allocation.
These studies thoroughly examine workload reallocation processes through DVFS across different DCCs. Nevertheless, the impact of flexibility potential provided by these workloads hosted on DVFS-enabled DCCs remains unexplored.

The power consumption of DCCs is primarily influenced by two components: static energy and dynamic energy~\cite{wang2017dvfs,hanumaiah2012energy,gu2014optimal,bani2017scalability}. The static energy term, often called idle power, arises mainly from the leakage current in the CPU core circuits. In contrast, the dynamic energy term is predominantly determined by the workload ratio $\lambda_{dcc}$ and the individual service level $\mu_{dcc}$. As such, the total power consumption of DCCs can be expressed as follows,~\cite{zhou2025data,lin2023workload},

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t} &= p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} + p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn},&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} &= v_{dcc,i,t} i_{leak,i},&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn} &= {\left( C_{dcc,i} f_{dcc,i} v_{dcc,i,t}^{2} \right )}{\lambda_{dcc,i,t}}{\mu_{dcc,i,t}^{-1}},\!\!\! & \forall i\in \mathcal{I}
  \end{align}
\end{subequations}

\noindent
% where $i$ and $\mathcal{I}$ represent the index and set of DCCs. $p_{dcc,i,t}$ denotes the total power consumption of individual DCC $i$. $p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle}$ and $p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn}$ represent the static (idle) and dynamic power consumption of DCC $i$, respectively. $C_{dcc,i,t}$ defines the constant representing the effective capacitance of DCC $i$. $f_{dcc,i,t}$ and $v_{dcc,i,t}$ denote the operational frequency and supply voltage of DCC $i$, respectively.

Based on the physical working principles above, the associated set of security constraints representing DCC workflows can be extended,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t} & \!\geq\! p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} \!+\! p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn},&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} & \!\geq\! \lfloor {v}_{dcc,i,t}\rfloor i_{leak,i},&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} & \!\leq\! \lceil {v}_{dcc,i,t}\rceil i_{leak,i},&\forall i \in\mathcal{I} \\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn} & \!\geq\! \lfloor ( C_{dcc,i,t} f_{dcc,i} v_{dcc,i,t}^{2} ) \rfloor {\lambda_{dcc,i,t}}{\mu_{dcc,i,t}^{-1}},&\forall i \in\mathcal{I} \\
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn} & \!\leq\! \lceil ( C_{dcc,i,t} f_{dcc,i} v_{dcc,i,t}^{2} ) \rceil {\lambda_{dcc,i,t}}{\mu_{dcc,i,t}^{-1}},&\forall i \in\mathcal{I}
    % \phi_{dcc,i}^{dvfs} &= ( C_{dcc,i} f_{dcc,i} v_{dcc,i}^{2} ), \forall i\in \mathcal{I}
  \end{align}
\end{subequations}

\noindent
where $\lceil {\circ} \rceil$ and $\lfloor {\circ} \rfloor$ represent predetermined upper and lower bounds for the decision variable $\circ$.
% $\lambda_{dcc,i}$ describes the workload balancing process for the DCC $i$. 
The feasibility of workload balancing for compute-intensive and storage-intensive workloads hinges on users' latency tolerance for batch workloads and the capacity to shift workloads among multi-DCCs,~\cite{gu2014optimal,hussain2022deadline}. When users accept and tolerate higher latency, the execution of workloads can be dynamically adjusted across multi-DCCs. By optimizing these workload execution patterns, the overall energy consumption of DCCs can be reduced, thereby enhancing their demand response capabilities for power system operations. More detailed, Fig.~\ref{fig:dvfs} illustrates the scheduled workload consumption generated by DCC servers with different support voltage and frequency parameter settings.

To estimate the benefits of DVFS-controlled DCCs in achieving smooth energy demand and proactive workload response, additional concise constraints regarding workload balancing processes among multiple DCCs are established,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
    \!\!\!\!p_{dcc,i,t} &\geq p_{dcc,i,t}^{idle} \!+\! p_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn} + \varDelta p_{dcc,i,t}^{bal},&&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\varDelta p_{dcc,i,t}^{bal} &= \phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} {\left ( \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini} \!+\! \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}\right )}{\mu_{dcc,i}^{-1}},&& \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} &= C_{dcc,i,t} f_{dcc,i,t} v_{dcc,i,t}^{2}, &&\forall i\in \mathcal{I} \\
    \!\!\!\!\lfloor f_{dcc,i} \rfloor &\leq f_{dcc,i,t} \leq \lceil f_{dcc,i} \rceil, &&\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\lfloor v_{dcc,i} \rfloor &\leq v_{dcc,i,t} \leq \lceil v_{dcc,i} \rceil, &&\forall i\in \mathcal{I}
  \end{align}
\end{subequations}

% \noindent
% where $\varDelta p_{dcc,i,t}^{bal}$ represents the change in the power consumption for DCC device $i$ due to workload balancing, $\lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini}$ represents the initial workload, and $\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}$ represents the adjusted workload.
% The term $\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs}$ represents the power regulation process for the DCC device $i$ through DVFS technique,
By incorporating the workload balancing dynamics defined in (3a)-(3c) into (2b) and (2c), the physical security constraints of DVFS-controlled DCC can be established.

In (3a), $\lambda_{dcc, i,t}^{bal}$ primarily depends on the risk of service time latency during workload reallocation processes. Generally, robust tolerance allows for a higher $\lambda_{dcc, i,t}^{bal}$, thereby enhancing the workload balancing capability of DCCs and enabling more flexible adjustment of workloads across DCCs. Nevertheless, an excessively long interval for workload reallocation would lead to prolonged service delays. Specifically, high-resource jobs continuously occupy specific DCC servers while smaller jobs pile up on others servers,~\cite{dayarathna2015data}. It may lead to a decline in overall resource utilization and a subsequent increase in service time latency. Fig.~\ref{fig:Qos} compares the service-time latency of workload reallocation with different intervals. To mitigate this, the metric of average workload queuing delay duration, $i.e.$, $T_{q,t} = \nicefrac{1}{(v_{dcc,i,t} - \lambda_{dcc,i,t})}$ is introduced to estimate the performance of workload balancing processes,~\cite{bani2017scalability,wang2015proactive}. The following outlines the related binding constraints concerning the index $T_{q,t}$,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
    \!\!\!\!T_{q,t} &\leq T_{tol}^{-1}\Rightarrow &\\
    \!\!\!\!\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{bal} &\leq v_{dcc,i,t} \!-\! T_{tol}^{-1} \!-\! (\lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini} + \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{dyn}), \!\!\!\!& \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{bal} &\geq 0, \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{bal}  \in \mathbb{R}, & \forall i\in \mathcal{I}
  \end{align}
\end{subequations}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.475\textwidth]{./fig/Qos.pdf}
\caption{The variability in service time latency during workload reallocation with different interval configurations.}\vspace{-0.25cm}
\label{fig:Qos}
\end{figure}

% where $T_{tol}$ represents the predefined threshold for the average workload queuing delay duration.

Notably, (3a)-(3c) remain intractable due to product terms involving continuous variables. Therefore, the McCormick envelope method,~\cite{leyffer2025mccormick}, is introduced to linearize them. Let $\iota_{dcc,i,t} = \phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}$, and additionally introduce two auxiliary variables $\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up}, \varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw}$ where $\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up} = \nicefrac{1}{2}(\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} + \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}) $, $\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw} = \nicefrac{1}{2}(\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} - \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}) $, accordingly, (3b) can be reformatted as its approximated linearized form as below,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
  \begin{align}
    \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\varDelta p_{dcc,i,t}^{bal} = &\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini}{\mu_{dcc,i,t}^{-1}} + \iota_{dcc,i,t} {\mu_{dcc,i,t}^{-1}} & \forall i\in \mathcal{I} \\
    \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\iota_{dcc,i,t} =&\phi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs} \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}, & \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\iota_{dcc,i,t} \geq &(\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up})^{2} - (\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw})^{2},& \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
    \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up} \geq & 0, \varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up} \in \mathbb{R}, \varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw} \in \mathbb{R},& \forall i\in \mathcal{I}
  \end{align}
\end{subequations}

In (4b), the standard square terms of $\langle \varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up},\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw}\rangle $ can be further reshaped as a Special-Ordered-Sets-of-type-1 (SOS1) constraint,~\cite{wilson2005algorithm}, which mix-integer-programming (MIP) solver could accurately identify and compile them. For simplification, let $\langle \varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up}, \varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw}\rangle$, respectively, denote the piece-wise block of related continuous variables $\langle \varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up},\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw}\rangle$, hence, (4b) can be reformulated as below,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
   \!\!\!\!\iota_{dcc,i,t} \geq & \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \Im(\varsigma_{dcc,i,t, z}^{up}) \varpi_{dcc,i,t, z}^{up} - \notag \\
   &\quad\, \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \Im(\varsigma_{dcc,i,t, z}^{dw}) \varpi_{dcc,i,t, z}^{dw}, & \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{up} & = \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up} \varpi_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up}, &\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\varsigma_{dcc,i,t}^{dw} & = \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw} \varpi_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw}, & \forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} &\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up} \!=\! 1,  \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw} = 1,\!\!&\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\Im(\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up}) &= (\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up})^2,  \forall z\in \mathcal{Z}, &\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\Im(\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw}) &= (\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw})^2,  \forall z\in \mathcal{Z}, &\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up} &\geq 0,\varsigma_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw}  \geq 0, \forall z\in \mathcal{Z}, &\forall i\in \mathcal{I}\\
   \!\!\!\!\varpi_{dcc,i,t,z}^{up} &\in\{0,\!1\},\varpi_{dcc,i,t,z}^{dw}\in\{0,\!1\}, & \forall z\in \mathcal{Z}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

It can be observed that constraint (3b) can continuously retreat as a product of two continuous variables termed as \(\pi_{dcc,i,t}^{dvfs}\) and \(\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t}\), and it can also be equivalent as a set of SOS1 constraint through the McCormick Envelope method. The detailed mathematical formulations for this approximation are provided in Appendix A. By combining these previously mentioned constraints, a comprehensive set of physical constraints describing workload power consumption can be effectively established.

\section{Interval-based workload balancing constraints formulation}

As previously discussed, \(\lambda_{dcc,i,t} \) serves as an indicator for the workload allocation process, where this process is achieved by regulating the operational frequency and supply voltage parameters of each DCC. Nevertheless, the overall workload must remain constant, even though the specific workload assigned to individual DCCs might be adjusted (increased or decreased). To capture this characteristic, the sum of \(\lambda_{dcc,i,t}\) for all workloads hosted on a specific DCC server should be restricted as follows,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  \sum\nolimits_{i\in\mathcal{I}} (\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t} + \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini} ) = \sum\nolimits_{i\in\mathcal{I}} \lambda_{dcc,i,t}^{ini},  &\forall i\in\mathcal{I} \notag\\
  \Rightarrow \sum\nolimits_{i\in\mathcal{I}} \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t} = 0,\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t} \in\mathbb{R}, &\forall i\in\mathcal{I}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Given that compute workload types differ across DCCs, and not all workloads can be freely transferable to other DCC servers, where this transferability is closely dependent on the current workload type. Specifically, only compute-intensive and storage-intensive types are generally transferable, while network-intensive types may not be. This necessitates an evaluation of each DCC flexibility potential capability to participate in workload reallocation and the establishment of the following constraints.

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  \lfloor \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i} \rfloor \leq \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t} \leq \lceil\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i} \rceil, \,\,\,\, \forall i\in\mathcal{I}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\noindent
where $\lceil {\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i}} \rceil$, $\lfloor {\varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i}} \rfloor$ denote the upper/lower bound of flexibility potential during workload balancing processes.
For these workload categories that can actively respond to the grid's flexible requirements, it is essential to balance local workload at the current period or adjacent periods rather than transferring them over too long a subsequent time interval. This prerequisite is necessary to avoid excessive user waiting time too much. To reflect this, additional restrictions must be constructed,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  & \sum\nolimits_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{k}}\sum\nolimits_{i\in\mathcal{I}} \varDelta \lambda_{dcc,i,t} = 0, \notag\\ 
  & \qquad \qquad \forall i\in \mathcal{I}, t\in\mathcal{T}, k\in\{1,2,3,\ldots, NK\}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Using this strategy, the entire scheduling horizon ($24$h) is segmented into a series of independent intervals such as $0$h-$6$h,$7$h-$12$h,$13$h-$18$h,$19$h-$24$h. \section{Conclusion}

This paper proposes a refined UC model that incorporates the flexibility capabilities of DCCs through DVFS techniques. Specifically, the flexibility potential gained from reallocating workloads across multiple DCC servers and the real-time adjustments of frequency and voltage parameters for individual DCC servers is investigated. Building on this, a set of physical security constraints representing these processes is extended. Furthermore, these constraints are reshaped as SOS1-derived linearized constraints and subsequently integrated into the scheduling framework.

Several findings in case studies suggested that the flexibility potential of workloads hosted by DVFS-controlled DCCs could be effectively exploited by adjusting the interval duration during which DCCs participate in workload reallocation. This work can reduce the overall operation cost of power systems without excessively delaying the service latency of workload response.


\section{Appendix A: Reformulation of constraints (3c) through McCormick Envelope Method}
Let $\gamma_{dcc,i}$ denotes the square value of \(v_{dcc,i}^{2}\), $i.e.$, \(\gamma_{dcc,i} = v_{dcc,i}^{2}\), the lower/upper bound of $\gamma_{dcc,i}$ can be obtained through estimating bindings parameters \((\lfloor v_{dcc,i} \rfloor)^2\) and \((\lceil v_{dcc,i} \rceil)^2\), respectively. This process holds because of the monotonic increasing property of the quadratic function. Accordingly, the constraints representing the lower/upper bounds of \(\gamma_{dcc,i}\)  can be derived as below,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  & \lfloor v_{dcc,i} \rfloor \leq v_{dcc,i} \leq \lceil v_{dcc,i} \rceil, & \forall i \in \mathcal{I}\\
  \Rightarrow \quad & (\lfloor v_{dcc,i} \rfloor )^2 \leq \gamma_{dcc,i}  \leq (\lceil v_{dcc,i} \rceil)^2, &\forall i \in \mathcal{I}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Next, $\phi_{dcc,i}^{dvfs}$ can be equivalently represented as the product of two continuous variables $\gamma_{dcc,i}$ and $f_{dcc,i}$. By utilizing the McCormick Envelope method, a reformulated, linearized approximation of this product, and thus of $\phi_{dcc,i}^{dvfs}$ can be constructed as,

\vspace{-0.25cm}
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
\kappa_{dcc,i}^{up} &= \nicefrac{1}{2}(\gamma_{dcc,i} + f_{dcc,i}), &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\kappa_{dcc,i}^{dw} &= \nicefrac{1}{2}(\gamma_{dcc,i} - f_{dcc,i}), &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\phi_{dcc,i}^{dvfs} &\geq \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \Im(\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{up})\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{up} -&& \\
&\quad\, \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \Im(\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{dw})\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{dw},&&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\kappa_{dcc,i}^{up} &= \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{up}\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{up},&&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\kappa_{dcc,i}^{dw} &= \sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} \kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{dw}\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{dw},&&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} &\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{up} = 1, &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\sum\nolimits_{z\in\mathcal{Z}} &\vartheta_{dcc,i,z}^{dw} = 1, &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\Im(\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{up}) &= (\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{up})^2, \forall z\in\mathcal{Z}, &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\Im(\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{dw}) &= (\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{dw})^2, \forall z\in\mathcal{Z}, &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}\\
\kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{up} &\geq 0, \kappa_{dcc,i,z}^{dw} \geq 0,\forall z\in\mathcal{Z}, &&\forall i\in\mathcal{I}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
